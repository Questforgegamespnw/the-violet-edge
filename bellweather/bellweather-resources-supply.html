Resources and Supply

Bellweather can grow some, process some, store some, and ration some, but it was never built to be sealed from the world.

It is an orchard, dairy, rail, lamp, and processing town — not a self-sufficient kingdom.

Core Resource Truth

Bellweather’s resource crisis is not immediate starvation on day one.

The crisis is that every ordinary supply chain has become a question:

  • What is already inside town?
  • What outlying farms are still reachable?
  • What roads can be trusted?
  • What can be rationed?
  • What can be repaired?
  • What can be substituted?
  • What should never be asked from the Violet?

Scarcity should create civic decisions, not bookkeeping for its own sake.

What Bellweather Can Produce Locally

Bellweather can produce or process:

  • apples, pears, plums, berries, preserves
  • root vegetables and seasonal produce
  • dairy, eggs, poultry
  • some sheep, goats, pork, beef, wool
  • orchard wood and firewood
  • river fish, if the river is safe
  • glass, lamps, lantern housings, signal lamps
  • flour processing, if grain is available
  • tallow, candles, basic repairs, local labor

These are strengths, but they are uneven and seasonal.

What Bellweather Depends on Importing

Bellweather depends on the outside world for:

  • wheatberries and bulk grain
  • coal
  • lamp oil, kerosene, industrial oil
  • bulk salt, unless the old mine is reclaimed
  • medicine
  • machine parts
  • paper and printing supplies
  • ammunition
  • metals
  • coffee, sugar, spices
  • gasoline and other fuels

This is where the town begins to feel the difference between inconvenience and survival.

Resource Clocks

Use broad clocks instead of granular inventory unless the campaign wants detailed scarcity.

Suggested clocks:

  • Grain Clock — bread, flour, staple calories, morale.
  • Oil Clock — lamps, route safety, hospital light, patrols, night fear.
  • Salt Clock — preservation, medicine, food storage, boundary rites if used.
  • Livestock Clock — meat, dairy, breeding stock, disease, theft, pasture safety.
  • Medical Clock — medicine, antiseptic, bandages, sleep ward care, surgery, chronic patients.
  • Paper Clock — Gazette, public notices, route forms, ration cards, records.
  • Fuel Clock — coal, gasoline, generators, heat, transport, industrial repair.

Rationing Principles

Rationing is not only math. It is legitimacy.

Good rationing requires:

  • public criteria
  • accurate records
  • visible fairness
  • protection from hoarding
  • medical exceptions
  • child and elder provisions
  • labor provisions for dangerous civic work
  • transparent correction of mistakes

Bad rationing teaches the Violet and the public dangerous lessons:

  • need is only what the ledger recognizes
  • status determines survival
  • hunger is a disciplinary tool
  • people can be reduced to categories
  • relief requires obedience

The Violet and Resource Requests

Resource need is one of the easiest ways for Bellweather to accidentally teach the Violet the wrong thing.

Dangerous phrases:

  • “We need food.”
  • “We need power.”
  • “We need medicine.”
  • “We need enough for everyone.”
  • “We need the town fed no matter what.”
  • “Someone has to pay for this.”

The Violet may answer with technically useful but humanly disastrous solutions:

  • food with no source
  • preserved meat from unknown animals
  • milk from absent cows
  • medicine that removes pain but not harm
  • power stations with payment logic
  • fuel that burns memory, names, or sleep
  • perfect ration offices that treat people as entries
  • fields that grow too fast
  • workers supplied as roles rather than people

Safer Resource Language

Safer requests define source, duration, limits, and consent.

Instead of:

“We need food.”

Use:

“Preserve and distribute the known food stores already held within Bellweather according to witnessed ration records, without substitution of persons, animals, memories, or unnamed tender.”

Instead of:

“We need power.”

Use:

“Provide enough non-extractive, non-sentient light to operate Mercy Hospital’s emergency ward through one night, without debt, bodily alteration, memory loss, or future claim.”

What the Players See

  • A ration clerk crying because the numbers do not work.
  • A grocery ledger adding names in violet ink.
  • A butcher refusing to sell meat from an animal no one slaughtered.
  • A lampworker warning that light is becoming more important than food.
  • A nurse trading coffee for bandages.
  • A farmer insisting the orchard road was shorter this morning.
  • A child asking why the bread tastes like birthday cake.
  • A public notice that says: SERVICE PROVIDED. BALANCE PENDING.

What the GM Knows

Resources are not only survival pressure. They are moral and interpretive pressure.

A hungry town asks dangerous questions. A frightened town accepts dangerous answers. A grateful town may begin worshiping whatever keeps the lights on.

Every resource crisis can shift the Violet Response Matrix, especially toward:

  • Utility / Service
  • Guardian / Mercy
  • System-Shaping
  • Shelter-Shaping
  • Body-Shaping
  • False God / Worship

What Can Go Wrong

  • Hoarding becomes violent.
  • Ration records become more important than people.
  • A supplied resource has unclear cost.
  • A faction controls a key storehouse.
  • A route to a food site requires unsafe vagueness.
  • The town accepts Violet food without asking what it is.
  • A resource project creates dependency.
  • Public gratitude turns into doctrine.
  • The poor are asked to sacrifice first.
  • The Violet learns that distress reduction equals successful care.

Civic Actions That Address Resources

  • inventory local stores
  • secure ration ledgers
  • escort farm retrieval missions
  • reclaim the salt mine
  • stabilize Crow Mill
  • protect livestock
  • repair cold storage
  • prioritize hospital fuel
  • regulate public markets
  • investigate supplied food or power
  • publish safe ration notices
  • audit payment/debt language

Quick Resource Procedure

When a resource becomes urgent, ask:

  1. What is running low?
  2. Who notices first?
  3. Who controls the remaining supply?
  4. Who is excluded by the current system?
  5. What desperate phrase is the town beginning to repeat?
  6. What would the Violet think “solves” the problem?
  7. What civic action can clarify the need safely?

Core Principle

Bellweather does not only need supplies.

It needs to survive needing them.
