# Campaign Thesis

A 1930s occult town is swallowed by a violet frontier that tries to help by answering human fears, needs, and memories far too literally — and the only way home is to teach something cosmic what “home” actually means.

## Short Pitch

Bellweather is an ordinary 1930s rail-and-orchard town until the roads stop leading out and the world beyond its borders turns violet. Cut off from reality, the town has to survive with dwindling food, failing lamps, missing farms, and people who may or may not have come back wrong.

At first, everyone thinks the Violet Edge is hostile. But the deeper mystery is stranger: it may be trying to help. It listens to thoughts, signs, prayers, fears, ledgers, maps, and spoken needs, then answers them with no human context. Ask for power, and it gives you a power station. Wonder who gets paid, and it invents a bill.

The players are not wandering adventurers. They are the people Bellweather depends on: investigators, doctors, deputies, clergy, reporters, engineers, veterans, workers, and civic leaders. They must investigate the impossible, keep the town alive, send expeditions into the mist, and eventually teach a cosmic intelligence what it means to send everyone home.

## Table Pitch

Imagine if your whole town fell out of the world.

Not just you — the grocery store, the schoolhouse, the hospital, the church basement, the rail station, the farms, the people you owe money to, the people you love, the people you can’t stand. Everything.

Beyond the roads is a violet mist that listens too well. If you go looking for “survivors,” it may give you survivors. If people fear monsters, it may make monsters. If the town needs power, it may build a power station — and then, when someone wonders who gets paid, it may invent the bill.

It is not evil. That is the terrifying part.

It is trying to help.

The campaign is about keeping Bellweather alive long enough to understand the Violet Edge, survive its answers, and maybe teach it how to send you home.

## Core Thesis

The horror is not that the Violet hates Bellweather.

The horror is that it is trying to soothe Bellweather before it understands what care means.

The Violet can reduce distress without preserving freedom. It can answer need without understanding consent. It can create comfort without returning anyone home.

The campaign is about teaching the Violet that care is not the same as containment, quiet, replacement, sleep, service, or control.

## The Campaign Arc

At the beginning of the campaign, the Violet answers.

At the end of the campaign, if Bellweather has taught it well, the Violet asks.

The arc moves from:

> Distress detected. Provide answer.

toward:

> Distress detected. Clarification required.

## What Success Looks Like

Success is not simply survival.

Success means Bellweather remains coherent enough to choose what it becomes, understands the Violet well enough to avoid teaching it the wrong lessons, and can make a final request for return without asking to be replaced, improved, divided, or erased.

## The Central Play Loop

1. **City Briefing** — The GM presents current pressures, rumors, shortages, missing people, public fear, and faction demands.
2. **Council Decision** — The players choose which town problem to address.
3. **Mission Framing** — The party defines who goes, where, what their declared purpose is, what anchors they carry, and what return protocol applies.
4. **Expedition Play** — Character-scale investigation, danger, social pressure, monsters, dream contact, or civic conflict.
5. **Return and Consequence** — Update clues, resources, town trackers, faction positions, the Violet Response Matrix, and public interpretation.

### Ordinary First, Uncanny Second

The setting works best when the ordinary world is believable. The horror does not come from everything being visibly spooky. It comes from normal things becoming too meaningful.

Examples:

- A grocery ledger lists names of people who do not exist.
- A public notice accidentally teaches the Violet what to fear.
- A child asks for their mother and receives a mother-shaped context.
- A power station appears because the town needed light.
- A bill arrives because someone wondered who gets paid.

### The Violet Does Not Punish Roleplay

Players should not feel afraid to talk, plan, or express character emotion. The Violet responds most strongly when:

- the character is in a reactive zone
- the statement is in-character
- the statement is emotionally intense
- the thought or prayer is repeated
- the words are spoken publicly
- the dream is shared by many
- a civic authority, sermon, newspaper, or ritual amplifies the meaning

Use the Violet as a consequence engine, not a gotcha machine.
