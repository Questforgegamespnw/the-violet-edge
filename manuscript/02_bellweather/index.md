# Bellweather Overview

Bellweather is not a backdrop.

It is the place the campaign is trying to save, understand, argue with, and return.

This section gives the GM and players the ordinary town before the Violet distorts it: its civic identity, daily routines, public institutions, supply chains, crisis pressures, and first survival questions.

Bellweather should feel practical before it feels impossible.

A road matters because people used it.

A school roster matters because children were expected there.

A lamp matters because someone maintained it.

A ration line matters because hunger has a public shape.

The Violet becomes frightening because it answers a town that already had names, habits, records, grief, labor, and obligations.

This section covers:

1. **Town Overview** — Bellweather's scale, civic identity, districts, institutions, motto, and local character.
2. **Daily Life** — what ordinary life looked like before and during crisis, including work, gossip, faith, school, class pressure, and public routine.
3. **Crisis State** — the town immediately after displacement: road failure, emergency governance, public fear, district states, and first civic questions.
4. **Resources and Supply** — what Bellweather produces, imports, stores, consumes, and risks losing as supply chains fail.

The goal is not to describe every shop, street, or resident.

The goal is to give Bellweather enough ordinary weight that every change to it hurts.

## Reading Use

Read this section before running the campaign's opening sessions.

Use it to establish:

- what Bellweather is like before the Violet becomes obvious
- what the public expects from civic authority
- which institutions matter
- what resources are immediately available
- what pressures appear first
- what ordinary language the Violet may begin answering

## Table Use

Player-facing material from this section can be shared freely with minor care.

GM-facing use should focus on what each ordinary institution can become under Violet pressure:

- Town Hall becomes a decision engine.
- The Gazette becomes public interpretation infrastructure.
- Mercy Hospital becomes the border between care and custody.
- The schoolhouse becomes a child-safety and identity anchor.
- The Lampworks becomes light, route, and boundary infrastructure.
- Grocers, mills, farms, and cold rooms become survival pressure points.

## Core Principle

> Bellweather must be ordinary enough to believe in before it becomes strange enough to fear.
