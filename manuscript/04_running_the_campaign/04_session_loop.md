# Session Loop

Bellweather works best with a repeatable session rhythm.

The players should feel that each session is not just an isolated mystery, but another day of the town trying to remain itself.

Use this loop as the default structure:

1. City Briefing
2. Council Decision
3. Mission Framing
4. Expedition Play
5. Return and Consequence

You can compress, expand, or skip steps as needed, but this loop keeps the campaign focused on both investigation and civic survival.

---

## How Long Is a Loop?

A City Mode loop is the period between major civic decisions.

It may represent:

- one urgent day
- several days of pressure
- a week of rationing and repairs
- the time between expeditions
- one major crisis cycle

Do not lock the loop to a strict calendar unless the situation calls for it.

Use the loop as a pacing tool.

The question is not always “How many hours passed?”

Instead:

> What did Bellweather have time, attention, labor, and trust to do before the next pressure arrived?

---

### 1. City Briefing

Begin with the town.

Present the current situation in practical terms.

Good briefing ingredients:

- one urgent resource issue
- one public rumor or interpretation problem
- one faction pressure
- one missing person or route concern
- one strange Violet development
- one possible expedition hook

Examples:

- Mercy Hospital has enough oil for one more night of full lamp use.
- The Gazette wants permission to print the word “miracle.”
- Three families demand a rescue mission to East Orchard Road.
- A ration ledger lists a household that no one remembers.
- Children at the schoolhouse drew the same locked room.

The briefing should create choices, not just mood.

### Briefing Atmosphere and Civic Pressure

The City Briefing should not feel like a sterile report.

Bellweather is a living town. Its pressure should be visible before the council ever votes.

Use the town’s current stress level, ignored problems, resource strain, and public interpretation to color the opening scene.

#### Calm / Contained

When the town is worried but still functional, show concern without disorder.

Examples:

- a few citizens waiting quietly outside the meeting room
- a mother asking whether the school will remain open
- a shopkeeper requesting clarification on ration rules
- a deputy whispering that people are asking about Eastgate
- hospital staff delivering a written request instead of interrupting
- clergy, union officers, or Gazette staff waiting to be called in

The feeling should be tense, but orderly.

People still believe someone is in charge.

#### Stressed / Publicly Fraying

When the town is stressed, let the pressure gather around the council.

Examples:

- a crowd in the hall demanding answers
- citizens barred from City Hall shouting outside
- families of the missing holding names and photographs
- union workers refusing night duty without oil priority
- parents demanding the schoolhouse be protected
- a preacher, rabbi, imam, or civic speaker warning that silence is making things worse
- Gazette reporters pressing for a public statement
- deputies visibly unsure whether to hold the line or let people in

The feeling should be political and emotional.

People are no longer only asking for reassurance. They are demanding decisions.

#### Critical / Near Breaking

When stress is critical, the council should feel surrounded by consequences.

Examples:

- City Hall doors locked against a crowd
- a faction leader forcing their way into the meeting
- a ration line turning violent nearby
- hospital bells ringing during the briefing
- a child witness brought in because adults ignored the issue too long
- a returned person appearing in the hall before anyone can explain them
- citizens chanting a dangerous phrase the Violet may answer
- a public notice torn down and rewritten by hand
- a trusted NPC accusing the council of choosing politics over people

The feeling should be that delay has become action.

If the council will not choose, the town will.

---

#### Ignored Problems Should Find a Voice

When the players repeatedly delay or ignore a problem, do not only mark a clock.

Bring the problem into the room.

A neglected issue may appear as:

- an important NPC demanding action
- a grieving family asking why no one has gone after the missing
- a faction using the delay to gain influence
- a public rumor becoming accepted truth
- an unsafe interpretation spreading through sermons, newspapers, schools, or ration lines
- a resource worker refusing further service
- a retainer asking whether the town intends to abandon their people
- a child, patient, clerk, or witness revealing that the problem has already worsened

This should not be used to punish players for prioritizing.

City Mode is built on impossible triage.

Instead, use ignored problems to show that every delayed issue continues living somewhere in Bellweather.

---

### Quick GM Prompt

At the start of each City Briefing, ask:

1. What did the council prioritize last loop?
2. What did they leave alone?
3. Who benefited from that choice?
4. Who suffered from that choice?
5. Who is now waiting outside the room?

---

### 2. Council Decision

The players decide what Bellweather prioritizes.

This may happen through an Emergency Civic Committee, informal faction council, player-character authority, public meeting, or practical triage.

Ask:

- What problem gets attention this loop?
- What problem is delayed?
- Who will be angry about that?
- What public explanation is given?
- What does the town think this decision means?

The decision should create both action and consequence.

---

## Council Slots

Bellweather cannot do everything every loop.

City Mode is not only about scarcity of food, oil, medicine, and labor.

It is also about scarcity of attention.

### Default Slot Economy

Each normal City Mode loop gives Bellweather **three Council Slots**.

Each Council Slot can hold:

- **one Major Civic Action**
- **one attached Supporting Action**
- optional character support
- optional resource spending

A Major Civic Action is a town-scale effort that requires meaningful authority, attention, labor, supplies, or public trust.

A Supporting Action is a smaller attached effort that improves, protects, clarifies, or limits the Major Action.

Not every Major Action creates an expedition. Expeditions happen only when the civic action must be resolved through direct character-scale danger, investigation, travel, or contact with the Violet.

### Adjusting Slot Count

Use three slots as the default.

Adjust when the town’s condition changes:

- **Quiet / recovery loop:** 2 Council Slots
- **Normal crisis loop:** 3 Council Slots
- **Emergency mobilization:** 4 Council Slots, but mark Labor/Fatigue or Public Trust
- **Collapse state:** 1–2 Council Slots until stabilized

Extra slots are not free.

If Bellweather over-mobilizes, someone gets exhausted, frightened, coerced, or ignored.

### Council Slot Template

```text
Major Action:
What is Bellweather trying to accomplish?

Lead Decision-Maker:
Which main character, institution, or faction takes responsibility?

Supporting Action:
What smaller effort improves or protects the action?

Personnel:
Who is assigned?

Resources Spent:
What clock, surplus, authority, or trust is committed?

Public Framing:
What does the town believe this action means?

Violet Risk:
What might the Violet misread?

Resolution:
Automatic, City Mode roll, character scene, or expedition trigger?

Result:
What changes afterward?
```

## Resolving Civic Actions

Use this procedure when a Council Slot requires a City Mode roll.

Use normal character rules when a specific character’s personal action is the dramatic focus.

Use City Mode when Bellweather acts through institutions, crews, public systems, factions, retainers, or civic projects.

City Mode uses **d10 dice pools**.

Each die showing **8, 9, or 10** is a success.

The size of the pool represents the town's current capacity, plus support, minus pressure.

---

### 1. Declare the Civic Action

Name the action clearly.

Define:

- objective
- lead decision-maker
- involved institution
- committed personnel
- committed resources
- public framing
- Violet risk
- return or completion condition

A civic action declaration is not a magical command, but it matters because Bellweather is inside the Violet.

A good declaration is specific, witnessed, limited, affirmative, grounded in records, clear about who is included, and careful about what it refuses to name.

A bad declaration is vague, fear-centered, punitive, category-based, publicly amplified without safer language, or too broad for the Violet to interpret safely.

---

### 2. Choose the Civic Capacity

Choose the most relevant town tracker.

The tracker rating becomes the base d10 pool.

Suggested civic capacities:

- **Survival** — food, water, medicine, shelter, sanitation, emergency care
- **Stability** — law, morale, trust, public order, legitimacy
- **Foothold** — maps, lamps, gates, routes, boundaries, district integrity
- **Understanding** — research, interpretation, occult/scientific knowledge, pattern recognition
- **Contact** — expeditions, missing people, outside signals, diplomacy, scouting
- **Adaptation** — new protocols, invention, training, altered methods, resilience
- **Return** — endgame anchors, home doctrine, continuity, final civic consent

Use a 0–5 rating.

```text
0 Broken
1 Fragile
2 Strained
3 Functional
4 Strong
5 Exceptional / deeply anchored
```

Example:

> Foothold 3 means Bellweather has a functional but stressed route, lamp, and boundary system. A civic action to stabilize Eastgate begins with 3d10.

---

### 3. Add Character Support

Each supporting character describes a concrete contribution and rolls an appropriate character skill.

Examples:

- A doctor supports hospital triage with Medicine.
- A Gazette editor supports public calm with Persuade or Psychology.
- A town clerk supports an identity audit with Library Use or Accounting.
- A deputy supports ration order enforcement with Intimidate, Law, or Firearms only if force is relevant.
- An engineer supports lamp-grid repair with Mechanical Repair or Electrical Repair.
- A clergy member supports grief discipline with Persuade, Psychology, Theology, Occult, or another table-approved faith/community skill.

Support roll outcomes:

- **Regular success:** +1 die to the City Mode pool
- **Hard success:** +2 dice
- **Extreme success:** +2 dice and reduce one consequence if the City Mode roll partially succeeds
- **Failure:** no bonus
- **Pushed failure:** no bonus and add a complication, strain, or Violet-risk tag
- **Fumble:** -1 die or automatic complication

Usually allow:

- one lead character
- up to two supporting characters

Allow more only if the action clearly has multiple workstreams.

Cap the total support bonus at **+3 dice** unless the group is spending a major resource or making this problem the session's main civic focus.

---

### When Everyone Helps

If every player wants to contribute, split the civic action into workstreams.

Example: **Stabilize Eastgate after a failed patrol.**

Possible workstreams:

- **Route Records** — clerk audits prior route declarations.
- **Physical Barrier** — engineers repair lamps, signs, and barricades.
- **Public Calm** — Gazette, clergy, or Town Hall explains the closure.
- **Patrol Readiness** — deputies organize guards and lantern-bearers.
- **Violet Interpretation** — occultist reviews what phrase, dream, or fear triggered the drift.

Each successful workstream may do one of the following:

- add +1 die
- remove -1 penalty
- protect against one specific consequence
- unlock a safer declaration
- identify the likely Violet misread before the final roll

Then roll one final City Mode pool.

Do not roll five separate full City Mode actions unless the problem is meant to consume the whole cycle.

---

### 4. Commit Resources

Spend relevant resources for extra strength.

- Spend 1 relevant resource: +1 die or remove one penalty
- Spend 2 relevant resources: +2 dice or protect against one likely consequence
- Spend a Surplus: +2 dice or +1 die and prevent one related consequence

Mark spent resources on their clocks.

Resource spending should matter in the fiction.

Examples:

- Spend Oil/Light for lit routes, hospital operations, gate watches, or night patrols.
- Spend Food for morale, labor strength, refugee care, or public calm.
- Spend Medicine for triage, recovery, sleep ward care, or surgery.
- Spend Paper/Records for notices, route forms, ration cards, audits, or identity records.
- Spend Labor for repairs, patrols, construction, searches, hauling, or barricades.
- Spend Public Trust for unpopular orders, curfews, ration enforcement, assembly limits, or secrecy.

A resource can also allow an action at all.

If the hospital has no medicine, a medicine-based civic action may be impossible without substitution, sacrifice, trade, or a dangerous Violet-facing request.

---

### 5. Apply Pressure

Subtract dice for active problems.

Common penalties:

- shortage
- public panic
- faction opposition
- rushed action
- weak identity
- vague declaration
- unsafe public language
- dangerous Violet interpretation
- exhausted labor
- missing records
- unreliable witnesses

Usually remove dice rather than changing the target number. Keep success on 8–10 so the procedure stays fast.

---

### 6. Roll the Pool

Roll the final d10 pool.

Each 8, 9, or 10 is a success.

- **0 successes:** failure with consequence
- **1 success:** partial success or success with cost
- **2 successes:** clean success
- **3+ successes:** strong success with extra benefit

### 0 Successes — Failure With Consequence

The action does not achieve its purpose, or achieves it in the wrong way.

Mark a relevant tracker down, spend the committed resource anyway, and introduce a Violet misread, faction consequence, public panic, or new trouble.

### 1 Success — Partial or Costly Success

The action works, but choose or assign a cost:

- resource spent
- time lost
- retainer harmed
- public fear rises
- interpretation shifts
- route becomes unstable
- faction gains leverage
- the Violet learns an unhelpful lesson

### 2 Successes — Clean Success

The action achieves its purpose without major new harm.

Update the relevant tracker or stabilize the pressure.

### 3+ Successes — Strong Success

The action succeeds and creates an additional benefit:

- extra information
- improved public trust
- reduced panic
- preserved resource
- better route
- faction support
- useful doctrine
- temporary Surplus
- reduced Dream Pressure
- a safer future declaration

---

## Town Stress and Complications

Town Stress does not only make failure more likely.

It makes success messier.

If Town Stress is low, ignore 1s unless the scene calls for danger.

If Town Stress is high, each die showing **1** creates a complication even if the action succeeds.

A complication may be:

- rumor spread
- public fear
- retainer injury
- faction backlash
- resource waste
- Violet misread
- witness exhaustion
- record corruption
- dream echo
- unsafe phrase repeated publicly

A stressed town can still succeed.

It just pays more for success.

---

### 7. Update the Town

After the roll, update:

- relevant civic tracker
- resource clocks
- public interpretation
- faction position
- Violet Response Matrix
- rumors, notices, or future trouble

Ask two final questions:

> What does this teach the public?

> What does this teach the Violet?

Practical success does not automatically mean metaphysical success.

---

## Resource Clocks

Use resource clocks instead of exact inventory unless the table wants detailed scarcity.

Suggested resource clocks:

- **Food / Grain**
- **Oil / Light**
- **Medicine**
- **Salt / Preservation**
- **Livestock**
- **Paper / Records**
- **Fuel / Coal**
- **Labor / Fatigue**
- **Public Trust**

A 6-segment clock works well.

```text
0–1 Stable
2–3 Strained
4–5 Critical
6 Crisis / shortage event
```

When a clock reaches 6, trigger a civic pressure.

Examples:

- **Food crisis:** ration unrest, theft, black market, hunger dreams.
- **Oil crisis:** route failure, hospital darkness, night panic.
- **Medicine crisis:** triage deaths, sleep ward failures, desperate Violet requests.
- **Paper crisis:** records degrade, route forms are rationed, identity anchors weaken.
- **Labor crisis:** accidents, resentment, exhausted witnesses, failed patrols.
- **Trust crisis:** faction revolt, underground sermons, rumor dominance.

After the crisis triggers, reset the clock to 3 if partially stabilized, or keep it at 6 if unresolved.

---

### Surplus

Surplus is a temporary advantage.

If a resource is in unusually good shape, mark it as Surplus.

Examples:

- Food Surplus
- Oil Surplus
- Medical Surplus
- Paper Surplus
- Labor Surplus
- Trust Surplus

A Surplus can be spent once for:

- +2 dice
- +1 die and prevent a related consequence
- allow a major project without ticking the resource clock
- cancel a related Critical penalty for one action

Then remove the Surplus.

Surplus should feel valuable, but not permanent.

---

### Shortage

If a resource clock is critical or maxed, it creates penalties.

- **Strained:** no penalty, but spending is risky.
- **Critical:** -1 die to actions relying on that resource.
- **Crisis:** the action may be impossible without substitute supply, sacrifice, trade, or a dangerous Violet-facing request.

Examples:

- Oil Critical: -1 to expedition, gate, hospital-night, and lamp-grid actions.
- Paper Critical: -1 to records, public notices, route declarations, and identity audits.
- Trust Critical: -1 to curfews, rationing, service bans, and public language campaigns.
- Labor Critical: -1 to patrols, construction, repairs, searches, hauling, and barricades.

---

## End-of-Loop Upkeep

At the end of each City Mode loop, ask what Bellweather relied on.

Mark 1 pressure on each resource that was heavily used:

- **Food**, if the loop covered feeding crowds, refugees, labor crews, or ration strain.
- **Oil / Light**, if routes, gates, hospital wards, or night patrols were active.
- **Medicine**, if there were injuries, illness, surgery, sleep cases, or panic casualties.
- **Paper / Records**, if the town issued notices, route forms, ration cards, audits, or identity records.
- **Labor**, if crews worked dangerous shifts, patrols, repairs, searches, or construction.
- **Public Trust**, if the town used secrecy, force, rationing, bans, curfews, or controversial orders.

If a resource was protected, replenished, or carefully managed during the loop, do not mark pressure.

If a resource was the focus of a successful civic action, reduce its pressure by 1 or create a temporary Surplus.

The GM does not need to run an economy every loop.

Ask what the town leaned on.

---

### 3. Mission Framing

Convert the chosen priority into playable scenes.

If the priority is civic, frame a City Mode scene.

If the priority requires leaving safety, frame an expedition.

If the priority is public meaning, frame a meeting, sermon dispute, Gazette debate, school assembly, hospital crisis, or faction confrontation.

For expeditions, define:

- party members
- destination
- purpose
- route
- witnesses
- anchors
- return phrase
- expected completion condition

For civic actions, define:

- responsible institution
- public declaration
- resources spent
- opposition
- tracker at stake
- possible misinterpretation

---

## Expedition Triggers

One loop usually produces **zero or one** expedition.

Two expeditions in one loop means Bellweather is stretching itself.

Three expeditions means something is going badly wrong.

An expedition is triggered only when the civic action cannot be completed from town and must be resolved through direct danger, investigation, travel, or contact with the Violet.

Before leaving City Mode, define:

- destination
- objective
- personnel
- route declaration
- lantern or anchor assignments
- public framing
- return condition
- what will happen if the party does not return

The city decides what must be done.

The expedition discovers what that decision costs.

---

### 4. Expedition Play

Run the main body of the session using normal character-scale play.

This may include:

- investigation
- interviews
- archives
- travel
- route drift
- combat
- negotiation
- dream contact
- medical crisis
- faction conflict
- resource recovery
- rescue
- Continuity Stress

Keep the ordinary world visible.

A strange event lands harder when it violates something concrete: a ledger, a bell, a ration line, a route form, a school roster, a hospital chart, a family photograph.

---

### 5. Return and Consequence

End by bringing the result back to Bellweather.

Ask:

- What did the players learn?
- What did they fail to learn?
- Who was helped?
- Who was harmed?
- What resource changed?
- What rumor spreads?
- What faction shifts?
- What tracker moves?
- What did the public now believe?
- What did the Violet now learn?

This step is essential.

Without return and consequence, Bellweather becomes a backdrop.

With it, the town becomes the campaign.

---

## Between-Session Updates

Between sessions, the GM can quietly update:

- clocks
- faction intentions
- public rumor
- missing person lists
- dream reports
- resource strain
- Violet response patterns
- future trouble escalation

Do not overprepare every possible branch.

Prepare pressure.

Let the players decide where the pressure breaks.

---

## Session Opening Template

```text
Since last session:
- [Resource or survival pressure]
- [Public meaning or rumor pressure]
- [Faction demand]
- [Violet response or strange sign]
- [Available civic action / expedition hook]
```

Example:

```text
Since last session:
- Mercy Hospital has opened a sleep ward.
- The Gazette has received three letters calling the Violet an angel.
- The Lampworkers’ Union refuses another night route without better oil priority.
- The Eastgate route board added Crow Mill in violet chalk.
- Town Hall needs a decision: ration oil, authorize the mill expedition, or regulate public sermons.
```

---

## Session Closing Template

```text
At the end of the session:
- [Clue learned]
- [Resource changed]
- [Person/faction affected]
- [Public interpretation shifted]
- [Tracker moved]
- [New Violet behavior]
- [Next pressure]
```

Example:

```text
At the end of the session:
- The party confirmed Crow Mill still exists, but its workers are counting shifts that never happened.
- Two barrels of flour returned safely.
- The mill foreman now distrusts Town Hall.
- Public belief shifts toward Utility / Service because the Violet-supplied machinery worked.
- Oil Clock drops by 1.
- A payment notice appears in the mill office.
- Next pressure: who pays for food the town did not produce?
```

---

## Pacing Guidance

A strong session usually needs:

- one town-scale decision
- one focused mission or civic conflict
- one meaningful clue
- one human cost
- one Violet misinterpretation or response
- one consequence that changes the next session

Do not try to resolve every tracker every session.

Pick the pressure that matters now.

---

## Core Principle

> Start with Bellweather.  
> Choose what the town can bear.  
> Leave with purpose.  
> Return with consequence.
