# Civic Actions

Civic actions are the town-scale things Bellweather can attempt when the players ask:

> What does the town do?

A civic action is not one person making one roll. It is a coordinated effort by institutions, crews, factions, retainers, public offices, and named decision-makers.

Civic actions are chosen during City Mode and resolved through the Session Loop when the outcome is uncertain.

They are how Bellweather turns fear into procedure.

---

## What a Civic Action Is

A civic action should define:

1. **Decision-Maker** — who authorizes or sponsors it.
2. **Objective** — what the action is meant to accomplish.
3. **Personnel** — who carries it out.
4. **Resources** — what supplies, authority, labor, records, or public trust are committed.
5. **Public Framing** — what the town believes the action means.
6. **Violet Risk** — what vague need, fear, or phrase could be answered badly.
7. **Return or Completion Condition** — what counts as success, failure, or safe closure.

A weak civic action says:

> We make the roads safer.

A stronger civic action says:

> Eastgate will suspend unrecorded departures, audit the last three route ledgers, assign two named witnesses to each patrol, and stabilize the Crow Mill route before another rescue is authorized.

The difference matters.

The Violet hears gaps.

## The Eight Major Civic Action Categories

### 1. Investigate the Mystery

Use when Bellweather needs to understand what is happening.

Examples:

- compare altered records
- interview returned people
- study a Violet-supplied building
- investigate shared dreams
- review old boundary maps
- identify a repeated public phrase
- collect witness statements
- analyze a new manifestation

Typical capacity: **Understanding**

Common risks:

- dangerous conclusions spread too early
- investigators mistake one pattern for the whole truth
- public language becomes contaminated
- the Violet answers the investigation method, not the goal

### 2. Survive Until Tomorrow

Use when the town needs immediate food, water, medicine, shelter, heat, sanitation, or emergency care.

Examples:

- ration food
- open a shelter
- triage the hospital
- repair a water line
- secure a cold room
- allocate coal and oil
- protect vulnerable residents
- organize sanitation crews

Typical capacity: **Survival**

Common risks:

- scarcity becomes a moral hierarchy
- rationing creates public anger
- the Violet tries to provide the missing resource too literally
- desperate people begin making unsafe requests

### 3. Solidify the Foothold

Use when Bellweather needs to make a place, route, district, or procedure more stable.

Examples:

- reinforce Eastgate
- repair ward lamps
- update maps and signs
- name unstable streets
- establish route offices
- protect district boundaries
- create witness posts
- mark safe return points

Typical capacity: **Foothold**

Common risks:

- over-defining a boundary excludes people outside it
- signs become too authoritative
- one district is protected while another is abandoned
- the Violet interprets “safety” as containment

### 4. Govern the Town

Use when the problem is order, legitimacy, morale, public language, law, rumors, or faction conflict.

Examples:

- issue a public notice
- hold a council hearing
- impose or lift curfew
- negotiate with a faction
- regulate unsafe assembly
- correct a rumor
- define ration rules
- decide who has authority

Typical capacity: **Stability**

Common risks:

- control replaces care
- public trust is spent too fast
- a faction exploits the decision
- the Violet learns that safety means obedience

### 5. Expand the Map

Use when Bellweather needs to know more of the Violet Edge, establish routes, scout resources, or confirm what still exists.

Examples:

- authorize a scouting patrol
- confirm a farm or mill
- map a boundary site
- test a road
- locate missing landmarks
- mark a new route
- identify other settlements
- expand lamp coverage

Typical capacity: **Contact** or **Foothold**

Common risks:

- route drift
- lost retainers
- dangerous discoveries
- public hope outruns proof
- the Violet fills map blanks with emotionally useful places

### 6. Build Power

Use when Bellweather develops new methods, training, infrastructure, rituals, tools, or countermeasures.

Examples:

- train witness clerks
- standardize route declarations
- repair generators
- build lantern housings
- develop dream protocols
- create recognition tests
- organize retainer squads
- improve hospital procedures
- establish communication relays

Typical capacity: **Adaptation**

Common risks:

- efficiency replaces consent
- systems become rigid
- tools are treated as wards instead of clarifications
- the Violet learns the form but not the human limit

### 7. Make Contact

Use when the town attempts to reach missing people, other settlements, entities, mediums, outside signals, or the Violet itself.

Examples:

- contact a missing patrol
- establish radio procedure
- parley with a returned person
- question a Violet-touched medium
- send a named rescue party
- attempt dream contact
- communicate with another settlement
- test whether the outside world can hear

Typical capacity: **Contact** or **Understanding**

Common risks:

- the contact answers too well
- grief overwhelms specificity
- unknown persons enter the town without identity protocol
- the Violet treats contact as invitation

### 8. Choose What the Town Becomes

Use when the issue is not practical survival alone, but identity.

Examples:

- decide who counts as Bellweather
- accept or reject a changed returnee
- preserve an old district name
- teach safer public language
- choose mercy over efficiency
- define what “home” must include
- decide whether Violet-supplied infrastructure may remain
- determine what the town is willing to become to survive

Typical capacity: **Return**, **Stability**, or **Understanding**

Common risks:

- the town teaches the Violet an exclusionary answer
- public meaning hardens around fear
- a moral compromise becomes civic doctrine
- Bellweather survives by becoming less itself

## Civic Reinforcement of Travel

Eastgate and other boundary checkpoints become more than barricades.

They become reality-stabilizing offices.

A proper departure is recorded by a clerk, repeated by the travelers, witnessed by named people, and entered into the town’s route ledger. This gives the Violet fewer gaps to complete incorrectly.

The clerk matters because the clerk turns intent into record.

The witness matters because the witness turns record into relationship.

The gate matters because the gate turns relationship into return.

A route declaration should ideally include:

- full names of all travelers
- destination
- purpose
- permitted route
- expected return gate
- lantern numbers
- assigned witnesses
- departure time
- return phrase
- physical tokens carried
- known risks or prohibited interpretations

The route is then read aloud before departure.

The travelers confirm it.

The witnesses confirm it.

The clerk records it.

The gate holds it.

This may look bureaucratic from the outside, but in Bellweather bureaucracy becomes survival magic by another name.

## Route Office Procedure

A mature Bellweather route office may use this procedure before any expedition.

1. **Name the Travelers**  
   Each traveler speaks their full name and confirms their role.

2. **Name the Destination**  
   The clerk reads the destination as written on the route form.

3. **Name the Purpose**  
   The party states the mission in one sentence.

4. **Name the Return**  
   The return gate, return phrase, and expected time are spoken aloud.

5. **Assign Witnesses**  
   At least two named witnesses confirm they will remember the route until it is closed.

6. **Record Lanterns and Tokens**  
   Lantern numbers, route tokens, maps, and personal anchors are logged.

7. **State the Limits**  
   The clerk reads any prohibited interpretations: no substitution, no unpaid tender, no dream custody, no replacement survivors, no altered return.

8. **Close the Declaration**  
   The travelers, witnesses, and clerk repeat the return phrase.

A route is not closed until the travelers return, are confirmed dead, are declared missing by civic vote, or the witnesses are formally released from holding the route.

## Focused Thought and the Waiting City

Bellweather also learns that waiting is active.

When a party leaves through Eastgate, the people who remain behind can strengthen the route by holding a consistent expectation:

> They are going to Crow Mill.  
> They are returning to Eastgate.  
> They are Mara Venn, Isaac Hale, and Ruth Bell.  
> They carry lanterns 3, 7, and 9.  
> They return by name.

This is not prayer in the usual sense.

It is civic attention.

Focused waiting becomes especially important during dangerous travel, rescue missions, dream expeditions, and attempts to recover missing people. The town does not need hundreds of people imagining every detail. That creates noise.

It needs a few named witnesses holding the same simple truth.

Too many conflicting hopes can destabilize the route.

A trained witness keeps the thought narrow:

> Named travelers.  
> Named destination.  
> Named return.  
> No substitution.  
> No addition.  
> No improvement.  
> Return by name.

## Public Notices as Civic Actions

A public notice is not flavor text.

Inside the Violet, official language can become infrastructure.

Use public notices to:

- clarify dangerous rumors
- announce ration rules
- stabilize route procedures
- define safe assembly limits
- protect children and missing persons
- explain service bans or emergency restrictions
- reduce vague public fear
- name what the town refuses to request

A good public notice is:

- short
- specific
- affirmative
- limited
- tied to records
- careful with metaphysical claims
- honest enough to preserve trust

A dangerous notice is:

- fear-centered
- punitive
- theologically absolute
- vague
- category-based
- written to control rather than clarify
- emotionally satisfying but interpretively unsafe

Example safer notice:

```text
EASTGATE TEMPORARY DEPARTURE ORDER

No person is to leave Bellweather through Eastgate without route registration, named witnesses, lantern assignment, and a stated return point.

This order protects return procedures.

It does not declare any missing person abandoned.

Routes remain open by civic authorization.
```

## Failed Witnessing

Witness systems can fail.

Common failures include:

### Conflicting Witnesses

Witnesses privately want different outcomes, and the route begins to split.

### Exhausted Witnesses

A witness falls asleep, panics, prays vaguely, or loses focus.

### Corrupted Record

A route form changes, smears, gains names, loses a destination, or lists an impossible return gate.

### Unwitnessed Departure

A party leaves without being named, recorded, or expected. The Violet has fewer constraints.

### Overloaded Declaration

Too many limits, fears, or desperate hopes are crammed into the declaration, creating interpretive noise.

### False Closure

The route is marked closed before the travelers truly return, making it harder for them to find the gate.

## What Can Go Wrong

Civic actions are powerful because they organize public meaning.

That also makes them dangerous.

Possible failures include:

- the action succeeds practically but teaches the Violet the wrong lesson
- a faction uses the action to gain authority
- a resource is spent faster than expected
- a public notice makes a dangerous phrase official
- one district is protected at another district’s expense
- clerks preserve a false record too well
- a route is stabilized around the wrong return condition
- compassion becomes an open invitation to vague returnees
- the town starts treating undocumented people as less real

The GM should not use these consequences as gotchas.

Use them when the action was vague, rushed, contested, emotionally charged, or publicly amplified.

---

## GM Use

When a civic action is proposed, ask:

- What specific problem is being addressed?
- Which tracker does it use?
- What resource or surplus is committed?
- Who is responsible if it goes wrong?
- What public language explains it?
- Who benefits?
- Who is left waiting?
- What does the Violet hear?
- Does this require a City Mode roll, a character scene, or an expedition?

If the answers are clear, the action can become a strong stabilizing force.

If the answers are vague, the action may still work.

It will just leave room for the Violet to help badly.

> A civic action is not just something Bellweather does.
