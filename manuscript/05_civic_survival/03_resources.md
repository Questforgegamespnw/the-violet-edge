# Resources

Bellweather does not begin the campaign empty-handed.

It has food, lamps, medicine, labor, records, tools, stores, and people who know how to keep a town running.

That is the point.

The crisis is not that everything is gone.

The crisis is that every supply chain has become a question.

Resources should create decisions, not bookkeeping.

---

## Resource Clocks

Use resource clocks instead of exact inventory unless the table wants detailed scarcity.

A 6-segment clock works well.

```text
0–1 Stable
2–3 Strained
4–5 Critical
6 Crisis / shortage event
```

Mark pressure on a resource clock when that resource is heavily used, wasted, damaged, stolen, rationed badly, or becomes central to public panic.

Reduce pressure when the town protects, replenishes, carefully rations, or successfully replaces that resource.

When a clock reaches 6, trigger a shortage event.

After the crisis triggers, reset the clock to 3 if the town partially stabilizes it, or keep it at 6 if the crisis remains unresolved.

## Core Resource Clocks

### Food / Grain

Includes stored grain, flour, preserved food, orchard produce, dairy, eggs, vegetables, livestock feed, and ration distribution.

Use for:

- feeding crowds
- supporting labor crews
- refugee care
- ration lines
- public morale
- long expeditions
- preserving children and patients

Crisis events:

- ration unrest
- theft
- black market
- hunger dreams
- families hiding food
- the Violet supplies food with unclear origin or cost

### Oil / Light

Includes lamp oil, kerosene, fuel for lamps, lanterns, hospital lighting, route lights, signal lamps, and emergency night work.

Use for:

- Eastgate procedures
- night patrols
- hospital operation
- route stabilization
- public safety
- ward lamps
- maintaining confidence after dark

Crisis events:

- hospital darkness
- route failure
- night panic
- unsafe candles and fires
- lantern theft
- the Violet provides light that does not need fuel but changes what it shines on

### Medicine

Includes bandages, antiseptic, surgical tools, sedatives, painkillers, quarantine supplies, hospital labor, and medical capacity.

Use for:

- triage
- injuries
- infection
- sleep cases
- birth and death care
- panic casualties
- expedition recovery

Crisis events:

- triage deaths
- quarantine conflict
- sleep ward failures
- desperate families seeking Violet help
- doctors making impossible choices
- medicine appears with impossible labeling or wrong patients attached

### Salt / Preservation

Includes food preservation, livestock care, old trade stores, curing, ritual use, and boundary folklore if the table uses that layer.

Use for:

- preserving meat
- protecting food stores
- stabilizing certain folk practices
- boundary rites
- medical and livestock needs
- winter preparation

Crisis events:

- food spoils
- butcher conflicts
- families hoard preserved goods
- salt becomes symbolically overvalued
- old salt routes become expedition hooks
- the Violet interprets preservation as keeping things unchanged forever

### Livestock

Includes cattle, pigs, chickens, horses, dairy animals, breeding stock, feed, barns, and animal health.

Use for:

- milk and eggs
- meat
- transport
- morale
- farm continuity
- leather, wool, and labor

Crisis events:

- disease
- strange births
- animals refuse a route
- livestock wander toward Violet pressure
- culling disputes
- the town argues over killing tomorrow’s survival for today’s food

### Fuel / Coal

Includes coal, wood, gasoline, generator fuel, stove fuel, industrial fuel, and heating.

Use for:

- heat
- machinery
- hospital power
- Lampworks production
- transport
- winter survival
- emergency manufacturing

Crisis events:

- cold shelters
- stalled machinery
- industrial shutdown
- carbon monoxide accidents
- fuel theft
- Violet-supplied power arrives with dangerous service logic

### Paper / Records

Includes paper, ink, forms, printing supplies, ledgers, route cards, ration tickets, school rosters, public notices, and archives.

Use for:

- route declarations
- ration cards
- identity audits
- public notices
- missing-person lists
- dream ledgers
- legal orders
- witness records

Crisis events:

- forms rationed
- records degrade
- official notices stop being trusted
- clerks become targets
- false documents gain authority
- the Violet generates perfect paperwork for false things

### Labor / Fatigue

Includes workers, deputies, nurses, clerks, lampworkers, drivers, farmers, teachers, witnesses, and everyone else asked to keep going.

Use for:

- repairs
- patrols
- construction
- searches
- hospital shifts
- route offices
- ration distribution
- public order

Crisis events:

- accidents
- exhaustion
- resentment
- witness collapse
- union refusal
- patrol failures
- shortcuts
- “volunteers” become coerced labor

### Public Trust

Includes legitimacy, patience, cooperation, belief that decisions are being made fairly, and willingness to follow painful instructions.

Use for:

- rationing
- curfews
- service bans
- secrecy
- difficult public notices
- quarantine
- route restrictions
- controversial rescue decisions

Crisis events:

- faction revolt
- underground sermons
- rumor dominance
- crowds at City Hall
- forged notices
- people leave without permission
- public language becomes careless or desperate

## Spending Resources

A resource can be spent during a City Mode action.

Spend 1 relevant resource to:

- add +1 die
- remove one penalty
- allow the action at all
- prevent one minor related consequence

Spend 2 relevant resources to:

- add +2 dice
- protect against one likely consequence
- make a large action possible
- support multiple workstreams

Mark the resource clock when it is spent.

Resource spending should be visible in the fiction.

If the players spend oil, lamps burn. If they spend labor, people work. If they spend public trust, someone accepts a hard order now and remembers it later.

---

## Surplus

Surplus is a temporary advantage.

If a resource is in unusually good shape, mark it as Surplus.

Examples:

- Food Surplus
- Oil Surplus
- Medical Surplus
- Paper Surplus
- Labor Surplus
- Trust Surplus

A Surplus can be spent once for:

- +2 dice
- +1 die and prevent a related consequence
- allow a major project without ticking the resource clock
- cancel a related Critical penalty for one action

Then remove the Surplus.

Surplus should feel valuable, but not permanent.

---

## Shortage

If a resource clock is critical or maxed, it creates penalties.

- **Strained:** no penalty, but spending is risky.
- **Critical:** -1 die to actions relying on that resource.
- **Crisis:** the action may be impossible without substitute supply, sacrifice, trade, expedition, or dangerous Violet-facing request.

Examples:

- Oil Critical: -1 to expedition, gate, hospital-night, and lamp-grid actions.
- Paper Critical: -1 to records, public notices, route declarations, and identity audits.
- Trust Critical: -1 to curfews, rationing, service bans, and public language campaigns.
- Labor Critical: -1 to patrols, construction, repairs, searches, hauling, and barricades.

---

## Upkeep

At the end of each City Mode loop, ask what Bellweather relied on.

Mark 1 pressure on each resource that was heavily used:

- **Food**, if the loop covered feeding crowds, refugees, labor crews, or ration strain.
- **Oil / Light**, if routes, gates, hospital wards, or night patrols were active.
- **Medicine**, if there were injuries, illness, surgery, sleep cases, or panic casualties.
- **Paper / Records**, if the town issued notices, route forms, ration cards, audits, or identity records.
- **Labor**, if crews worked dangerous shifts, patrols, repairs, searches, or construction.
- **Public Trust**, if the town used secrecy, force, rationing, bans, curfews, or controversial orders.

If a resource was protected, replenished, or carefully managed during the loop, do not mark pressure.

If a resource was the focus of a successful civic action, reduce its pressure by 1 or create a temporary Surplus.

The GM does not need to run an economy every loop.

Ask what the town leaned on.

## Violet Resource Misreads

The Violet hears need.

Resource shortages are therefore dangerous.

When the town publicly says:

> We need food.  
> We need power.  
> We need medicine.  
> We need safety.  
> We need workers.  
> We need the dead remembered.

the Violet may answer the literal, symbolic, emotional, or civic meanings of those words.

Examples:

- Food arrives, but it is tied to debt, offering, replacement, or living source.
- Power arrives as electricity, authority, control, debt service, or obedience.
- Medicine arrives as sleep, sedation, preservation, or removal of distress.
- Safety arrives as locked doors, sealed streets, or people kept from leaving.
- Workers arrive as role-made entities, false returnees, or exhausted dead.
- Memory arrives as records that refuse to let grief change.

Resource actions should always define limits.

A safer resource declaration names quantity, purpose, duration, source constraints, consent, and end condition.

## Civic Action: Secure a Resource

Use when the town attempts to gain, protect, ration, transport, reclaim, or substitute a critical resource.

Define:

- the resource
- current clock state
- who needs it most
- who controls it
- who will object
- what it will be spent on
- what is not acceptable as a substitute
- what happens if it fails

This action may be resolved through City Mode if it can be handled internally.

It may trigger Expedition Mode if the resource is outside stable Bellweather.

> Scarcity is not only what Bellweather lacks.  
> Scarcity is what Bellweather is tempted to ask for badly.
