# Dr. Helena Cross

Dr. Helena Cross is trying to keep bodies alive while the town argues over meanings.

She has no patience for poetry when patients are bleeding, sleeping, changing, or returning wrong.

## Background

Helena Cross was born in Bellweather, left for medical training, and came back when Mercy Hospital needed a physician who could do more than keep records and pray. Depending on the campaign's desired pressure, she may have trained in Seattle, Portland, Chicago, or Boston before returning to a town that still remembers her as someone's sharp-tongued daughter.

She returned because Bellweather needed her, but also because outside institutions made it very clear how far a woman doctor could rise before doors began closing politely. Mercy Hospital gave her authority. The Violet crisis now threatens to turn that authority into a cage.

## Appearance

Helena is in her early forties, severe when tired, with dark hair usually pinned back too tightly and sleeves rolled with practical impatience. Ink, iodine, or coal-smudge often marks her cuffs. Her eyes are quick and clinical until someone is suffering in front of her.

## Physical Impression

She moves with brisk economy, as if every wasted step costs medicine. Helena does not hover. She examines, decides, and moves on. Her bedside manner is blunt but attentive; her cruelty, when it appears, is usually disguised as efficiency.

She has a habit of removing her glasses before saying something emotionally honest.

## Family and Personal Ties

Helena may have aging parents in Bellweather, an estranged sibling who thinks she became too proud, or a fiancé/husband/partner outside town whose absence she refuses to discuss. She is fiercely protective of nurses and orderlies because she knows whose labor keeps hospitals alive.

Nurse Clara Hobb, if used, should be the person most able to tell when Helena is about to make a bad call.

## Public Reputation

To patients, Helena is frighteningly competent. To grieving families, she is sometimes too cold. To Town Hall, she is indispensable and difficult. To some older residents, she is still the girl who left and came back with city airs.

## Leadership Style

Helena leads through triage, evidence, quarantine, and controlled access. She is willing to be disliked if it keeps patients alive. Her moral danger is that she can reduce personhood to medical continuity: pulse, respiration, chart, symptom, risk.

## Private Contradiction

Helena believes suffering must be reduced, but the Violet keeps offering examples where distress decreases while personhood becomes uncertain.

Part of her fears that, under enough pressure, she might accept that bargain.

## Quick Portrayal

**First Impression:** A doctor running out of sleep before she runs out of orders.  
**Voice:** Crisp, low, impatient with vagueness.  
**Posture:** Forward-moving, sleeves rolled, hands already reaching for work.  
**Usual Location:** Mercy Hospital, sleep ward, quarantine room, records office.  
**Known For:** Saving people and refusing comforting lies.  
**Soft Spot:** Nurses, children, patients who apologize for needing care.  
**Hard Line:** Families interfering with triage or quarantine.  
**Pressure Trigger:** Being asked to call something healed because it is quiet.  
**If She Breaks:** She preserves bodies and loses track of people.

## Public Face

Hospital director. Precise. Tired. Controlled. More compassionate than she sounds.

She wants quarantine, supplies, scientific control, and the authority to say “no” when families demand impossible comfort.

## Private Pressure

One patient remembers a different Bellweather.

Helena has not told everyone how much that frightens her.

## What She Wants

- medical supplies
- quarantine protocols
- sleep ward control
- honest triage
- protection for staff
- authority over returned patients
- clear distinction between pain relief and healing

## What She Knows or Suspects

- some Violet help removes distress without treating harm
- dream and sleep cases are increasing
- medical records may preserve continuity
- families will accept dangerous answers if grief is not managed

## What She Can Offer

- medical authority
- patient records
- triage staff
- autopsy reports
- quarantine wards
- grounded skepticism
- evidence of body-shaping or memory discontinuity

## Under Stress

Helena may reduce people to symptoms, cases, or contamination risks.

Her worst version teaches the Violet that bodies should be stabilized even if personhood is uncertain.

## Signal Leak Risk

> “If I can stop the suffering, does it matter what they remember?”

## GM Use

Use Helena when care and control collide. She is an excellent voice for practical necessity that may still become morally dangerous.
