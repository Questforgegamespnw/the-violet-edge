# Elias Crow

Elias Crow is old enough to remember when people still lowered their voices around certain roads.

Most people thought that made him a crank.

Now they are less sure.

## Background

Elias Crow is tied to Crow Mill by blood, debt, labor, or all three. His family may have owned the mill once, lost it to creditors, or kept it alive through stubborn maintenance no one thanked them for.

He understands machinery, grain, weather, and worker resentment. Before the crisis, he worried about invoices and rail delays. Now he worries whether flour, labor, and the road to the mill still mean the same things.

## Appearance

Elias is in his fifties or sixties, flour-dusted, long-faced, and spare. His hands are strong despite age, with cracked knuckles and nails that never fully come clean. He dresses in work clothes under an old coat that still carries mill dust in the seams.

## Physical Impression

He speaks slowly, often after doing the arithmetic in his head twice. Elias can seem miserly until you see just how much of his caution is survival math.

He listens to machines the way doctors listen to lungs.

## Family and Personal Ties

Elias may have a niece, son, or nephew among the missing orchard routes, or a family split by inheritance, union pressure, or debt. He has long-standing friction with grocers, farmers, and town officials because everyone needs the mill and no one wants to admit what it costs.

## Public Reputation

The town sees him as stingy, dour, necessary, and probably hiding something. Farmers accuse him of taking too much. Workers accuse him of demanding too much. Town Hall asks him for miracles with broken machines.

## Leadership Style

Elias leads through control of process: what comes in, what gets milled, what is lost, what is rationed, what can wait. He is not charismatic, but his numbers are often right.

His danger is that scarcity can make him think in categories before names.

## Private Contradiction

Elias believes waste is immoral, but Bellweather may force him to decide whether mercy is inefficient.

## Quick Portrayal

**First Impression:** A flour-gray millman doing grim arithmetic while everyone else argues.  
**Voice:** Dry, slow, suspicious.  
**Posture:** Stooped from labor, eyes sharp over ledgers.  
**Usual Location:** Crow Mill, grain stores, Market Street ration meetings.  
**Known For:** Hard numbers and harder answers.  
**Soft Spot:** Workers who know machines, hungry children, old debts quietly forgiven.  
**Hard Line:** Wasting grain for political comfort.  
**Pressure Trigger:** Being asked to feed everyone with food that does not exist.  
**If He Breaks:** He lets the ration category replace the person.

## Public Face

Old farmer. Crank. Survivor. Memory-holder. Hard to like quickly, harder to ignore.

He wants rescue for his family and seed grain preserved.

## Private Pressure

Elias knew the Violet Edge existed before Bellweather vanished.

He did not know it could take the whole town.

## What He Wants

- rescue his family
- preserve seed grain
- protect Crow Mill
- force Town Hall to admit old warnings mattered
- keep practical survival ahead of politics
- decide how much old knowledge to reveal

## What He Knows or Suspects

- some roads were never just roads
- the old names matter
- the mill sits on a meaningful site
- the Violet may answer barter, boundary, and memory differently than modern civic language

## What He Can Offer

- old route knowledge
- food and mill access
- boundary stories
- family records
- hard practical advice
- warnings about old mistakes

## Under Stress

Elias may withhold information until his family is prioritized.

His worst version teaches the Violet that care is barter and memory is leverage.

## Signal Leak Risk

> “I warned them. Let them pay attention now.”

## GM Use

Use Elias when the campaign needs old-town memory, resource stakes, and an uncomfortable witness to Bellweather's buried past.
