# Jonah Pike

Jonah Pike has no patience for leaders who discover the dignity of labor only when the lights go out.

He is not anti-town. He is anti-sacrifice dressed as civic necessity.

## Background

Jonah Pike is a senior lampworker, crew boss, or ward-light engineer whose practical knowledge keeps Bellweather from becoming dark by sundown. He apprenticed young, learned glass, brass, oil flow, route lamps, and emergency repair the hard way, and has the burn scars to prove it.

Before the crisis, he fought with management over pay, hours, and safety. After the crisis, everyone suddenly discovered lampworkers were essential.

## Appearance

Jonah is in his forties or fifties, broad-handed, soot-marked, with old burns on his wrists and forearms. He smells faintly of oil, hot metal, tobacco, and rain-soaked wool. His work coat has pockets full of wire, striker tabs, pencil nubs, and spare screws.

## Physical Impression

He occupies space like a man used to shouting over machines. Jonah gestures with tools and thinks with his hands. He distrusts clean boots in a repair site.

When worried, he starts checking lamps that are already working.

## Family and Personal Ties

Jonah may have a spouse, grown children, or younger siblings tied to the Lampworks district. His crew is family in the way dangerous labor makes family: arguments, loyalty, grudges, and shared scars.

He knows which managers cut corners and which workers can be trusted with a lantern outside the gate.

## Public Reputation

Workers trust him. Town Hall finds him difficult. The public thinks of him as gruff but reliable until the lights fail, at which point they treat him like a saint or a scapegoat.

## Leadership Style

Jonah leads through competence, direct orders, and loyalty to crew safety. He will do dangerous work if the need is real. He will not spend workers as if they are tools.

His danger is that he may define the town by what can be repaired, powered, lit, or made functional.

## Private Contradiction

Jonah knows light protects, but he has begun to fear Bellweather may worship whatever keeps darkness back.

## Quick Portrayal

**First Impression:** A soot-streaked foreman who can fix almost anything except panic.  
**Voice:** Loud, blunt, practical.  
**Posture:** Sleeves rolled, weight forward, tools in hand.  
**Usual Location:** Lampworks, Eastgate lamps, hospital power rooms, repair yards.  
**Known For:** Keeping the lights on and swearing at impossible machinery.  
**Soft Spot:** Injured workers, apprentices, anyone holding a lamp wrong but trying.  
**Hard Line:** Sending exhausted crews into unstable routes without names.  
**Pressure Trigger:** Officials treating labor as an abstract resource.  
**If He Breaks:** He asks for power without asking what powers it.

## Public Face

Union organizer. Blunt. Protective. Good with workers, bad with polite committees.

He wants no disposable labor crews.

## Private Pressure

His workers found old symbols under the factory floor.

He has not yet decided whether to tell Town Hall, Isaac Venn, the Gazette, or no one.

## What He Wants

- worker safety
- ration fairness
- no unrecorded night crews
- recognition for labor losses
- protection for worker families
- investigation of the symbols
- control over how many crews are risked

## What He Knows or Suspects

- the Lampworks is older than its official records suggest
- light does something more than illuminate
- poor districts will be sacrificed first if no one fights it
- replacement workers would be the beginning of the end

## What He Can Offer

- crews
- repair capacity
- labor intelligence
- fuel knowledge
- access to Lampworks spaces
- direct pressure on Town Hall

## Under Stress

Jonah may shut down essential repairs or mobilize workers against the Committee.

His worst version teaches the Violet that reciprocity means a ledger of grievance.

## Signal Leak Risk

> “If they want light, let them pay what it costs.”

## GM Use

Use Jonah when the players need to confront whether their survival plan treats workers as people or inputs.
