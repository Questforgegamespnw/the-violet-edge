# Ruthie Marr

Ruthie Marr is not a teacher, prophet, or monster.

She is a child of Bellweather who notices what adults step over.

Depending on campaign needs, Ruthie can be a student at the schoolhouse, a River Ward child, a missing child who returned wrong, or the central child tied to the Violet House trouble.

## Background

Ruthie Marr is a child of Bellweather, not a prophecy machine. She knows the town from below adult eye level: which fences have loose boards, which shopkeepers give broken candy, which graves are scary, which roads feel mean, and which grownups lie with kind voices.

Before the crisis, adults knew her as imaginative, stubborn, quick to wander, and hard to fool. After the roads failed, her drawings and questions began mattering more than any adult was comfortable admitting.

If tied to the Child in the Violet House trouble, Ruthie may be the child who was found, the child who vanished, the child who keeps drawing the house, or the friend who insists the missing child is not afraid.

## Appearance

Ruthie is eight to ten years old, small for her age or all knees and elbows depending on portrayal, with scuffed shoes, quick hands, and hair that refuses to stay fixed. She may carry a school slate, ribbon, marble, doll, lunch tin, or folded drawing.

Her clothes should look lived in: patched stockings, ink on her fingers, a too-large coat, a ribbon tied by someone in a hurry, or a lunch pail with her name scratched into it.

## Physical Impression

She watches before speaking. When comfortable, she asks direct questions adults avoid. When frightened, she becomes very literal, because literal rules feel safer.

Do not portray her as eerie by default. Let her be a child first: hungry, curious, stubborn, bored, observant, occasionally selfish, and capable of kindness.

## Family and Personal Ties

Ruthie's family situation should be chosen to fit the table's comfort and the intended trouble web. She may have living parents, a missing mother, an older sibling on patrol, a widowed father, or a guardian who is trying and failing.

She should have at least one adult who loves her imperfectly and one adult she trusts for reasons that are inconvenient to the plot. That trusted adult might be Miss Clara Whitcomb, Sister Agnes Rowe, Deputy Mara Venn, Dr. Helena Cross, or a family member the council cannot easily reach.

## Public Reputation

Adults may call Ruthie imaginative, difficult, sensitive, or too observant. Other children may follow her because she knows good hiding places. Teachers may worry because her drawings become accurate before they should.

The public should not agree on what she means. Some people think she is traumatized. Some think she is gifted. Some think children should be kept far away from council business. Some quietly want to use her as a detector.

## Leadership Style

Ruthie does not lead officially. She influences through attention, imitation, questions, games, and repetition. Children copy her language faster than adults notice.

Her danger is not that she is powerful. It is that the Violet responds strongly to children because need, fear, and care are so clear around them.

## Private Contradiction

Ruthie wants adults to tell the truth, but she also wants the truth to be small enough to sleep near.

She can be brave about impossible things and terrified of ordinary ones: being forgotten, being sent away, adults whispering, someone not coming home for supper.

## Quick Portrayal

**First Impression:** A child with scuffed shoes noticing the thing adults stepped over.  
**Voice:** Plain, literal, sometimes startlingly direct.  
**Posture:** Watchful, fidgeting with a token, sleeve, slate, or folded drawing.  
**Usual Location:** Schoolhouse, church basement, Market Street, family porch, hidden shortcuts.  
**Known For:** Drawings, questions, and remembering what adults said yesterday.  
**Soft Spot:** Smaller children, lost animals, adults who apologize honestly.  
**Hard Line:** Being told not to ask because she is little.  
**Pressure Trigger:** Adults lying about whether someone is coming back.  
**If She Breaks:** Her need for comfort becomes a shape the Violet can answer.

## Public Face

Child witness. Student. Neighborhood child. Someone adults want to protect, dismiss, use, or explain away.

She should not be made responsible for saving Bellweather. She is evidence that Bellweather’s choices reach children first.

## Private Pressure

Ruthie draws accurate maps of places she has never visited.

Some drawings show rooms, roads, houses, or doors before adults know they exist. Some show the Child in the Violet House smiling.

## What She Wants

- adults to tell the truth
- someone to keep promises
- children not to be used as bait or detectors
- to know whether the missing are scared
- to keep one ordinary routine intact
- to believe home still knows her name

## What She Knows or Suspects

- adults lie differently when they are scared
- some places feel like they are waiting to be named
- children repeat phrases before adults notice those phrases matter
- drawings can show what the Violet is preparing to answer, not clean prophecy

## What She Can Offer

- child testimony
- drawings
- overheard adult conversations
- playground routes and hidden shortcuts
- names children use for places before adults officialize them
- early warning signs of dream pressure
- emotional truth adults are avoiding

## Under Stress

Ruthie may hide, lie to protect another child, repeat a dangerous comfort phrase, or seek safety in a place the Violet can complete too well.

Her worst version does not become evil. Her worst version teaches the Violet that a child who is quiet, warm, and no longer frightened has been helped.

## Signal Leak Risk

> “I just want somebody to come get me and never let anything bad happen again.”

## GM Use

Use Ruthie when the town's future, children, maps, care, and memory collide. She should be one of the strongest emotional anchors in Bellweather, but not a solution engine.

Ruthie is there to make the council remember who their decisions are for.
