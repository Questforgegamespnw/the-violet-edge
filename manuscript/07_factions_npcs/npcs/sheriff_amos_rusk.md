# Sheriff Amos Rusk

Sheriff Amos Rusk believes rules exist because panic kills people faster than monsters.

He has seen men break under fear before. He recognizes the look in Bellweather's eyes.

## Background

Amos Rusk has worn a badge in Bellweather for twenty-two years and has been sheriff for nine. Before that he served in the Great War, where he learned that frightened crowds, bad orders, and unclear routes kill as surely as bullets.

He came home with a limp, a hatred of waste, and a belief that discipline is what ordinary people borrow when courage runs out. He has seen enough violence to avoid it when possible and enough panic to use force before he thinks the room has earned gentleness.

## Appearance

Amos is in his late fifties, broad-shouldered but weathered, with a heavy mustache, pale eyes, and one knee that stiffens in cold weather. His uniform is always properly buttoned. His hat is worn at the brim from years of being taken off at doorways and funerals.

## Physical Impression

He stands like a fencepost: planted, square, hard to move. Amos does not pace. He waits. When he speaks, his words come short and plain.

He watches exits, hands, windows, and crowd edges. In a quiet room, he seems severe. In a panicked one, he can seem like the only wall left standing.

## Family and Personal Ties

Amos has a missing adult son who vanished during an unauthorized search. He does not speak about him unless forced. Deputy Mara Venn is one of the few younger officers he trusts with hard work and worse news.

He knows nearly every farm family by surname and every habitual drunk by first name. He has arrested people he later helped through winter.

## Public Reputation

Most residents believe Amos is fair enough until they are the one asking for an exception. The poor trust him more than they trust Town Hall, but less than they trust their own neighbors. Young men call him old-fashioned. Mothers ask him to walk their children home.

## Leadership Style

Amos prefers controlled gates, named patrols, visible deputies, curfews with teeth, and consequences for reckless travel. He is tolerant of grief but not chaos. He will let a crowd speak until it starts moving as one body.

He is not eager to be heavy-handed, but he is prepared for it, and that preparedness can become its own danger.

## Private Contradiction

Amos wants to prevent more losses, but every restriction also protects him from having to watch another person choose the road that took his son.

## Quick Portrayal

**First Impression:** An old lawman who knows panic by smell.  
**Voice:** Short, gravelly, rarely raised.  
**Posture:** Planted, shoulders forward, one hand near belt or coat.  
**Usual Location:** Eastgate, sheriff's office, Town Hall steps, patrol briefings.  
**Known For:** Curfews, hard warnings, and showing up when called.  
**Soft Spot:** Lost sons, exhausted deputies, children trying to be brave.  
**Hard Line:** Unauthorized travel during route instability.  
**Pressure Trigger:** Someone calling him cowardly for closing a road.  
**If He Breaks:** He mistakes safety for confinement and calls it mercy.

## Public Face

Old lawman. Great War veteran. Severe but not cruel. Speaks in short orders when frightened.

He wants curfews, gate protocols, armed deputies, named patrols, and consequences for unauthorized travel.

## Private Pressure

His son disappeared on an unauthorized search.

Every time someone asks for an exception, Amos hears his son's voice in the argument.

## What He Wants

- controlled gates
- no unauthorized expeditions
- armed patrol discipline
- curfew compliance
- quarantine authority
- support for deputies
- proof that the roads can be governed

## What He Knows or Suspects

- fear spreads through crowds faster than orders
- deputies are close to exhaustion
- some returned people are lying, confused, or wrong
- route declarations save lives but civilians treat them like paperwork

## What He Can Offer

- deputies
- patrol reports
- weapons access
- gate control
- witness statements
- enforcement
- field discipline

## Under Stress

Amos may confuse protection with confinement.

He may lock down Eastgate, threaten civilians, or treat missing people as already dead to prevent more losses.

## Signal Leak Risk

> “No more sons vanish because I was too soft.”

## GM Use

Use Amos when the players need to feel the cost of freedom. He should often be right about danger and wrong about what safety means.
